Building on Boids to Mimic Murmurations

How we extended the classic three-rule algorithm to simulate 3,000+ starlings sweeping across a twilight sky.

Starting Point: Classic Boids

Craig Reynolds' 1986 Boids algorithm shows that three local rules — separation, alignment, and cohesion — are enough to produce convincing flocking. Each agent looks at its neighbors, computes a steering force from each rule, and adjusts its velocity. No central planner, no global knowledge. The resulting school of fish (or flock of birds) emerges on its own.

The Boids Playground on this site demonstrates that beautifully with 10 fish. But murmurations are a different beast: thousands of starlings moving in dense, fast, wave-like formations. Scaling up exposed several problems — and the solutions turned out to be interesting in their own right.

Our Approach: Local Rules, Emergent Behavior

The genius of Reynolds' original algorithm is that no bird knows what a "flock" is. Each bird follows simple local rules — react to nearby neighbors — and the flock emerges as a side effect. There's no conductor, no blueprint, no global plan. The whole is something that none of the parts intended.

We wanted to preserve that principle. Every modification we made to the classic three rules — topological neighbors, split neighbor counts, adaptive centroid pull, front-bias hemisphere weighting — introduces or adjusts only local rules: what a single bird perceives, and how it reacts to what it perceives. No bird is told where the flock should go or what shape it should take.

We also tried to ground each rule in at least plausible biology. Topological neighbors come directly from field research on real starlings. Split neighbor counts reflect an intuition that collision avoidance and heading-matching operate at different spatial scales. The adaptive centroid pull models a bird's awareness that "I seem to be alone" — something a real starling almost certainly notices and reacts to. Front-bias weighting captures the idea that birds may pay more attention to what's ahead of them than what's behind.

The one exception is wind. It's a uniform external force, not a local rule — the same push applied to every bird regardless of its neighbors. We include it because without some source of directional instability, the flock tends to hover in place, and real murmurations sweep and drift. Wind is a proxy for whatever global-scale mechanism actually causes that movement — atmospheric currents, collective momentum, or something we don't yet understand. It's the one place where we cheat, and we flag it as such.

The payoff for this discipline is that the behaviors that emerge — coherent sweeping waves, dense cloud-like formations that stretch and compress, breakaway clusters that rejoin the group — weren't designed. They arise from local interactions the same way real murmurations do. And they correlate strongly with what you see watching actual starlings at dusk.

Problem 1: Scale

The fish playground checks every pair of fish for neighbor interactions. With 10 fish that's 45 pairs — trivial. With 3,000 birds it's nearly 4.5 million pairs per frame. At 60 frames per second, that's over a quarter billion distance checks per second. Even modern hardware can't keep up.

Spatial hashing

The fix is a spatial hash grid. We divide 3D space into cells (10 units on a side) and drop each bird into the cell that contains it. To find a bird's neighbors, we only check the 27 cells in its 3×3×3 neighborhood — typically 30–80 candidates instead of 3,000. The hash is rebuilt from scratch every frame (clear all cells, re-insert all birds), which sounds expensive but is actually O(n) and very fast.

InstancedMesh rendering

The fish playground creates a separate 3D mesh for each agent. With 3,000 birds that means 3,000 draw calls per frame — enough to tank any GPU. Instead, we use Three.js's InstancedMesh: one geometry (a 12-vertex icosahedron), one material, and a single draw call that stamps it at 3,000 different positions. The birds look like small grey dots, which turns out to be exactly what real starlings look like at murmuration distance.

Structure-of-Arrays data layout

Instead of 3,000 Boid objects each containing a position vector and a velocity vector (which scatters data across the heap and triggers garbage collection), we use six flat Float32Arrays — one each for px, py, pz, vx, vy, vz. This keeps the data cache-friendly and avoids GC pauses entirely. No objects are allocated during the simulation loop.

Problem 2: Who Are Your Neighbors?

Classic boids use a fixed radius for each rule: if another bird is within, say, 6.5 units, it's a neighbor for alignment. This is called metric interaction. It works fine for small groups, but it has a scaling problem: in a dense flock, a bird might have 200 neighbors inside its radius, while a bird at the edge has 3. The work per bird varies wildly, and the behavior changes with density.

Biologists studying real starling murmurations (Cavagna et al., PNAS 2010) discovered that starlings don't use a fixed radius. Instead, each bird interacts with its nearest 6–7 neighbors regardless of how far away they are. This is called topological interaction. The number of interacting neighbors stays constant whether the flock is dense or sparse, which is what makes murmurations scale-free — a flock of 300 looks structurally similar to a flock of 3,000.

We adopt this approach, but with a twist: different neighbor counts for different rules.

Split neighbors

Separation only needs to know about the closest few birds — you dodge what's right in front of you. So we use a small N (default 7) for the separation rule. But alignment and cohesion benefit from a wider view — matching heading with more neighbors creates longer-range coherence and the large-scale wave propagation that gives murmurations their visual character. So those rules use a larger N (default 14).

The spatial hash query finds the largest N requested; the separation rule simply only considers the closest subset. Since findNearest returns neighbors sorted by distance, this is free — no extra work.

The Forces, in Detail

Each frame, every bird computes a steering acceleration from seven sources. These are summed, applied to velocity, and velocity is clamped between a minimum and maximum speed (birds can't hover, and they can't exceed a sprint speed).

1. Separation

For each of the nearest Nsep neighbors that's closer than SEP_DIST (1.5 units), compute a repulsion vector pointing away from that neighbor, weighted by the inverse of the distance (closer = stronger push). Average the repulsion vectors, then steer toward that direction.

repulsion += (myPos - neighborPos) / distance
steer toward average repulsion, scaled by W_SEP

The distance threshold means separation is self-limiting — it only fires when birds get uncomfortably close, and the topological neighbor count for separation can be different (smaller) than for the other rules.

2. Alignment

Average the velocity vectors of all Nali/coh neighbors. Steer toward that average heading. This is the dominant force in a murmuration — it's what makes the flock move as a coherent mass, and it's what propagates turn waves across the group.

avgVelocity = sum(neighbor velocities) / N
steer toward avgVelocity, scaled by W_ALI

3. Cohesion

Average the positions of all Nali/coh neighbors. Steer toward that center point. This keeps the flock together — without it, alignment alone would send birds marching off in formation but never pulling back together.

center = sum(neighbor positions) / N
steer toward (center - myPos), scaled by W_COH

4. Seek flock centroid (adaptive)

This is where things get interesting — and where we depart most from classic boids. Each bird steers gently toward the centroid (center of mass) of the entire flock, computed each frame by averaging all bird positions. But the strength of this pull depends on how isolated the bird is.

We measure isolation by the average distance to the bird's nearest neighbors. A bird deep in the flock (average distance < 2 units) is surrounded — it gets barely any centroid pull (~0.05). A bird that's isolated or in a small breakaway cluster (average distance > 8 units) gets strong homing force (~0.8).

isolation = clamp((avgNeighborDist - 2) / 6, 0, 1)
pullWeight = 0.05 + isolation * 0.75
steer toward centroid, scaled by pullWeight

This creates two distinct behavioral phases from a single continuous rule:

Dense flock (murmuration state): centroid pull is negligible. Pure boids rules govern — alignment propagates waves, cohesion maintains density, separation prevents collisions. The murmuration sustains itself.

Isolated birds: centroid pull dominates. Stray birds and broken-off clusters are drawn back toward the main group. The murmuration reforms.

This mirrors a real intuition about murmurations: the mechanisms that maintain the formation (local neighbor-matching) may differ from those that form it (seeking the group). Rather than switching algorithms, the adaptive weight creates a smooth phase transition between the two regimes.

5. Wind drift

A gentle uniform acceleration applied to every bird — the same direction and strength for all. The direction wanders randomly, smoothly lerping to a new target every 5–12 seconds. This gives the entire flock net momentum so it sweeps across the sky rather than hovering in place. Vertical drift is damped to 40% of horizontal, because real wind is mostly lateral.

6. Boundary

A soft quadratic push-back that activates when a bird enters the boundary margin (20 units from the edge of a 200×140×160 unit arena). The force grows with the square of penetration depth, so it's barely noticeable at the edge but becomes a hard wall deeper in. This keeps the flock in view without visible bouncing.

7. Ground plane

A checkerboard floor sits beneath the flock to give a sense of altitude and motion. Birds feel a soft upward push that begins 40 units above the ground — double the normal boundary margin — giving them plenty of room to turn away gradually.

The push is velocity-aware: a bird diving toward the ground feels extra resistance proportional to its downward speed, so it banks away in a smooth arc rather than bouncing off a wall. A bird cruising horizontally near the ground barely notices anything extra. This mirrors real birds, which see the ground coming and adjust their trajectory long before impact.

Both the positional push and the velocity-damping term scale with the flock's max speed setting, so even at high speeds the ground remains effective without needing to be tuned separately. A hard floor clamp at the checkerboard surface acts as a last-resort safety net, but the soft forces handle virtually all cases.

The Steer Function

All of the above forces (except boundary and wind) use the same steering formula, adapted from Reynolds' original work. Given a "desired direction" vector:

desired = normalize(desired) * MAX_SPEED
steer = desired - currentVelocity
steer = clamp(steer, MAX_FORCE)

This computes the velocity change needed to fly at maximum speed in the desired direction, then caps the magnitude at MAX_FORCE (0.5). The cap is crucial — it means birds can't make instant turns. They have inertia. A high MAX_SPEED (15) with a moderate MAX_FORCE (0.5) produces wide, sweeping arcs — exactly the character of starling flight.

Starting in the Murmuration State

An interesting question we deliberately set aside: how does a murmuration form? When hundreds of scattered birds converge into a single coherent mass, are the mechanisms the same as those that sustain it once it's underway? We suspect not — formation likely involves different dynamics than maintenance, perhaps analogous to a phase transition where the governing rules themselves shift as the system crosses a density or alignment threshold.

Rather than trying to capture both regimes, we focused on maintenance: the mechanisms that keep a murmuration coherent once it's already in its dynamic state. This is what we actually observe when we watch starlings at dusk — by the time we notice the murmuration, it's already underway. The question of how it got there is fascinating, but it's a separate modeling challenge.

To start in-state, the simulation initializes with conditions that are already murmuration-like:

  • Tight spawn: all birds start inside a sphere whose radius scales with the cube root of the bird count (r = cbrt(n) * 0.6). This keeps initial density roughly constant regardless of flock size, and ensures the neighbor graph is fully connected from frame one.
  • Common heading: a random direction is chosen for each reset, and all birds start flying that way with small per-bird jitter (30% random deviation). The flock is already aligned and moving — you're joining a murmuration in progress.

The adaptive centroid pull (described above) provides a bridge between these regimes: isolated birds experience strong homing force, while birds already in the flock are governed by local rules alone. This isn't a full formation model, but it does allow the flock to recover from fragmentation — a necessary property of any robust murmuration simulation.

What We Learned

The classic boids rules — separation, alignment, cohesion — are necessary but not sufficient for murmuration dynamics. The extensions that mattered most:

  • Topological neighbors instead of metric radii. This matches the biology and makes the algorithm scale-free.
  • Split neighbor counts — small N for separation, larger N for alignment and cohesion. Different rules need different awareness ranges.
  • Adaptive centroid pull — the key insight. Isolated birds get strong homing force; birds already in the flock get almost none. This creates two behavioral phases (formation and maintenance) from one smooth function.
  • Starting in-state — rather than trying to form a murmuration from random initial conditions, we begin in the murmuration phase and let the algorithm maintain it. This mirrors reality: murmurations are observed, not assembled.
  • Speed matters — fast birds with moderate steering caps produce wide, graceful arcs. Slow birds look like they're drifting in soup.

That said, real murmurations exhibit behaviors this simulation doesn't fully capture: the dramatic shape-shifting, sudden splits and recombinations, and cascading turn waves that propagate faster than the birds fly. Those phenomena likely arise from predator avoidance (a hawk diving into the flock triggers a panic wave) and possibly from information transfer mechanisms beyond simple velocity matching. That's a frontier for future work.
